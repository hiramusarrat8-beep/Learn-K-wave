Start with the reason

Why do we need simulation in the first place?

The wave is hidden after it enters the body.

On the outside we know the transducer position and the drive signal. Inside the tissue, the wave is bent, reflected, absorbed, and delayed by structures we cannot directly see during experiment. Simulation gives researcher a way to predict the pressure field before they trust a setup in the lab.

Outside view We can see the setup, not the pressure field.

The aberration problem — corrected explanation

The wave travels transducer -> gel -> skull -> brain There are two refraction events: at the gel-skull interface and at the skull-brain interface. The skull-brain exit is especially important because the beam must arrive correctly focused inside the intended target region.

When a wave crosses from skull, around 2900 m/s, into brain, around 1560 m/s, it slows down and bends. This bending can shift the focal spot away from the intended target. This is aberration.

What is the acoustic wave and why is stimulation difficult?

Transcranial focused ultrasound relies on the propagation of acoustic pressure waves through tissue. The central challenge is that the skull is heterogeneous and highly attenuating. Cortical bone has a sound speed nearly double that of brain tissue, creating impedance mismatch at gel-skull and skull-brain interfaces. These mismatches cause partial reflection and spatial aberration, so the focal spot can shift away from the intended target. k-Wave helps by modeling each voxel with its own acoustic properties, often derived from image data.

From brain image to simulation grid
1

Acquire a CT or MRI scan

CT measures X-ray attenuation at each location and returns Hounsfield Units (HU). Air = −1000 HU, water = 0 HU, dense cortical bone ≈ 1000 HU. The image is a 3D grid of these numbers.

2

Segment the image into tissue regions

Software such as SimNIBS or FreeSurfer identifies which voxels belong to skull, brain, CSF, and scalp. Each voxel receives a tissue label. This step is called segmentation.

3

Assign three acoustic properties to every voxel

Each voxel's tissue label or raw HU value is converted to speed of sound c, density ρ, and attenuation coefficient α. Brain voxels can use reference tissue-property values. The result is three matrices, all the same size as the original image.

4

Hand the matrices to k-Wave

k-Wave uses numerical arrays to define the acoustic environment. Each voxel is treated as one grid cell with its own speed of sound, density, and attenuation values. During the simulation, the pressure and particle velocity fields are updated across this voxel-wise map, so understanding acoustic property maps is the entry point for any patient-specific simulation.

Each square represents one voxel. The colour is its speed of sound. This is literally what medium.sound_speed looks like as a matrix.

Layer 2 · Heterogeneous Medium

Voxel-wise Acoustic Properties

The core idea

Simple simulators assume a uniform medium — same speed and density everywhere. k-Wave assigns every single voxel its own acoustic properties, making it a heterogeneous medium. This is what allows accurate simulation of how sound behaves differently in skull bone versus brain tissue versus trabecular marrow.

Three properties you assign per voxel

1. Speed of sound (c) — m/s

Determines how fast the wave travels through that voxel. At boundaries where c changes abruptly, the wavefront bends according to Snell's Law. This bending is the primary cause of focal shift in transcranial ultrasound.

Water / gel 1500 m/s Brain tissue 1560 m/s Skull cortical 2900 m/s

2. Density (ρ) — kg/m³

Together with c, gives acoustic impedance: Z = ρ × c (in MRayl). When Z changes between two voxels, part of the wave reflects back. The coupling gel between transducer and skin exists purely to eliminate the air–skin Z mismatch, which would otherwise reflect ~100 % of energy.

Water 1000 kg/m³ Brain 1040 kg/m³ Skull cortical 1900 kg/m³

3. Attenuation coefficient (α) — dB / (MHzy · cm)

Determines energy loss per unit distance. Units are dB per MHz-to-the-power-y, per centimetre. The exponent y is set separately via medium.alpha_power. Skull bone attenuates roughly 20–25× more than brain tissue, which is why higher frequencies are far harder to use transcranially.

Brain ~0.6 dB/MHzy/cm Skull cortical ~10–15 dB/MHzy/cm
How it looks in k-Wave
% Scalar (uniform) — simplest case
medium.sound_speed = 1500;          % m/s, whole grid
medium.density     = 1000;          % kg/m³

% Map (heterogeneous) — voxel-wise, same size as kgrid
medium.sound_speed  = c_map;        % [Nx × Ny] array
medium.density      = rho_map;      % [Nx × Ny] array
medium.alpha_coeff  = alpha_map;    % dB / (MHz^y · cm)
medium.alpha_power  = 1.1;          % y exponent, typically 1–2
k-Wave medium struct — every field explained
c
medium.sound_speed scalar or Nx×Ny matrix

Speed of sound in m/s. Scalar = uniform. Matrix = each voxel has its own value. Controls wave speed, refraction angle, and the k-space correction operator κ.

medium.sound_speed = 1500 + ones(Nx, Ny);  % then overwrite skull columns
p
medium.density scalar or Nx×Ny matrix

Density in kg/m³. Combined with sound_speed to give acoustic impedance Z = ρc at each voxel — controls how much reflects at tissue boundaries.

medium.density = 1000 + ones(Nx, Ny);
α
medium.alpha_coeff dB / MHzy / cm

Power law absorption coefficient. Controls energy loss to heat. Skull absorbs 4000× more than water per unit path length. Measured from ex vivo tissue samples — not derived from patient images (unlike c and ρ for skull).

medium.alpha_coeff(:, skull_cols) = 11;  % skull cortical
y
medium.alpha_power scalar only

Power law exponent. α(f) = α₀ · f y. Water: y≈2. Soft tissue: y≈1.1. Skull: y≈0.9. Must be scalar — same power law applies to all tissues.

medium.alpha_power = 1.1;  % tissue-like
B/A
medium.BonA optional — nonlinearity

Parameter of nonlinearity. Enables simulation of harmonic generation at high pressures. Tissue B/A ≈ 6–8. Water B/A ≈ 5. Leave unset for linear simulations.

medium.BonA = 6;  % enable nonlinear simulation

CT to acoustic property conversion (Aubry 2003)

This is the most cited formula in tFUS literature. CT measures Hounsfield Units (HU): air = −1000 HU, water = 0 HU, dense cortical bone = ~1000 HU. 

% Step 1: Convert HU to porosity
porosity = 1 - (HU_map / 1000);
% porosity = 0  →  pure dense bone  (HU = 1000)
% porosity = 1  →  pure water / air (HU = 0)

% Step 2: Map porosity to acoustic properties (Aubry 2003)
medium.sound_speed = 2900 - 1480 .* porosity;   % m/s
medium.density     = 1900 - 900  .* porosity;   % kg/m³
medium.alpha_coeff = 13.3  .* porosity + 0.17;  % dB/(MHz^y cm)
medium.alpha_power = 2.0;

% Step 3: Override brain / water regions
medium.sound_speed(brain_mask) = 1560;
medium.density(brain_mask)     = 1040;
medium.alpha_coeff(brain_mask) = 0.6;

Physical intuition: At porosity = 0 (pure dense cortical bone) → c = 2900, ρ = 1900. At porosity = 1 (pure water) → c = 1420, ρ = 1000. The formula linearly interpolates between bone and water values based on how porous the voxel is.

New from source papers — Perfectly Matched Layer (PML)

★ Source-verified — PML

k-Wave surrounds the entire grid with a Perfectly Matched Layer (PML) — by default 20 grid points thick in 2D, 10 in 3D. Because the FFT assumes the pressure field is periodic, a wave hitting the right edge would reappear on the left (wave wrapping). The PML absorbs outgoing waves before this happens. Critical rule: never place your source or sensor inside the PML. Your usable grid is Nx minus 2 × PML_SIZE points per dimension.

★ Source-verified — staggered grids

k-Wave uses spatially and temporally staggered grids. Pressure is computed on the regular grid; particle velocities are computed on grids shifted by half a grid spacing (Δx/2) in each direction. This is why velocity inputs/outputs in k-Wave are spatially and temporally offset from pressure. If an interviewer asks why source.ux and source.p behave differently — this is why. 

% PML accounting in practice
PML_X_SIZE = 20;               % default in 2D
Nx = 128 - 2*PML_X_SIZE;      % usable grid = 88 points
% The PML occupies the outer 20 rows on each side
% Do NOT place source / sensor inside these 20 points
Putting it together
"k-Wave assigns each voxel its own speed of sound, density, and attenuation coefficient derived from CT Hounsfield units. Following the Aubry 2003 approach, HU is first converted to skull porosity by normalising against the maximum bone value of 1000 HU, and then speed of sound and density are linearly interpolated between cortical bone values — 2900 m/s and 1900 kg/m³ — and water values using that porosity. Attenuation follows a frequency power law with units of dB per MHz-to-the-power-y per centimetre, where the exponent y is set separately. The grid is also surrounded by a Perfectly Matched Layer to prevent wave wrapping at the domain boundaries."

Layer 3 · Numerical Engine

k-Space Pseudospectral Method

The big idea in one sentence
Instead of computing how pressure changes point-by-point using finite differences (slow, inaccurate), k-Wave transforms the entire pressure field into spatial frequency space (k-space) via FFT, computes the spatial derivative there as an exact multiplication by ik, then transforms back. This is the pseudospectral method.
Why finite differences fail — the walking analogy

Imagine walking and at each step you mis-estimate your direction by 1 degree. After 10 steps: 10 degrees off. After 300 steps: completely lost. Finite difference makes a small approximation error at every time step. After thousands of time steps the wave appears to travel at the wrong speed — the focal spot drifts. k-Space makes no approximation in the spatial derivative — so no error accumulates.

Property Finite difference k-Space pseudospectral
How derivatives computed Local (2 neighbour points) Global (FFT of entire field)
Error accumulation Yes — phase drift over time No — dispersion-free*
Grid points per wavelength ≥10 required ~2–3 sufficient
Memory for 3D sim >400 GB (unusable) Feasible on normal hardware

* Exactly dispersion-free for linear, lossless, homogeneous media. See nuance below.

The FFT explained — why spatial derivatives become multiplications

The Fourier Transform answers: "what spatial frequencies are hiding in this pressure pattern?" Instead of "frequency in Hz" applied to time, we get k = wavenumber in cycles/metre applied to space. That's where the name k-Wave comes from.

p(x) → FFT → P(k)

Spatial pressure field → k-space representation (spatial frequencies)

In k-space, a spatial derivative becomes a simple multiplication — because the derivative of a sine wave is a cosine, which in complex notation is multiplication by i:

∂p/∂x → ik · P(k)

Exact. No approximation. Works for every grid point simultaneously.

The 5-step process k-Wave uses every time step:

% At each time step, k-Wave does this for spatial derivatives:
% 1. Take FFT of pressure field
P_k = fft(p_field);

% 2. Multiply by ik (= exact spatial derivative in k-space)
dP_dx_k = 1i .* k_vec .* kappa .* P_k;

% 3. Inverse FFT → exact derivative at every grid point
dp_dx = real(ifft(dP_dx_k));

% 4. Update particle velocity (momentum conservation)
ux = ux - (dt/rho0) .* dp_dx;

% 5. Repeat for all Cartesian directions (y, z)
The k-space operator κ — source verified exact formula

κ = sinc(cref · k · Δt / 2)

The k-space operator. Applied as a correction factor to every time step.

★ Nuance from source papers — when κ is exact vs approximate

The k-space operator κ is exactly dispersion-free only for linear, lossless, homogeneous media. When the medium is heterogeneous (like skull + brain), the correction is no longer exact because cref is a single scalar applied globally, but local sound speeds vary. However, it still dramatically reduces numerical dispersion compared to standard finite differences. For skull simulations, cref is typically set to the maximum sound speed in the medium (~2900 m/s for skull). The paper confirms: "for soft biological tissue where medium parameters are weakly heterogeneous, the k-space method remains an apposite numerical technique."

About 60% of k-Wave's total computation time is spent performing forward and inverse FFTs — this is why GPU acceleration (DataCast = 'gpuArray-single') and the C++ version (kspaceFirstOrder3DC) exist for large 3D simulations.

CFL stability condition — must know for MATLAB

CFL = cmax · dt / dx < 0.3

Courant–Friedrichs–Lewy condition. k-Wave default CFL = 0.3 using cmax in the medium.

% k-Wave calculates dt automatically based on CFL
% kgrid.dt = CFL * dx_min / c_max

% Example: skull simulation
dx     = 0.5e-3;      % 0.5 mm grid spacing
c_max  = 2900;        % skull speed of sound
CFL    = 0.3;

dt = CFL * dx / c_max;
% dt ≈ 52 nanoseconds

% Nyquist rule — at least 2 points per wavelength
f0      = 500e3;      % 500 kHz
c_brain = 1560;
lambda  = c_brain / f0;   % = 3.12 mm
% dx must be ≤ λ/2 = 1.56 mm
% We chose 0.5 mm — well within limit ✓

% Set time array manually if needed:
kgrid.makeTime(medium.sound_speed, CFL, t_end);
⚠ Nuance from source papers — grid points per wavelength

The k-space method achieves spectral accuracy approaching the Nyquist limit of 2 PPW, whereas finite differences require ≥10 PPW. The k-Wave manual confirms classical methods need 10+ PPW for "useful accuracy" — making 3D simulations computationally impossible at diagnostic frequencies without the k-space approach. In practice, labs use 3–6 PPW for safety margin, especially with heterogeneous skull media.

"Pseudospectral" — what the word actually means

Spectral = spatial derivatives computed in frequency/k-space (exact). Pseudo = time stepping still done in real space (not fully spectral). k-Wave is a hybrid: spatial operations are spectral and exact, time stepping is real-space with a κ correction that removes numerical dispersion. This is fundamentally different from purely spectral methods.

Interview answer — explain the k-space pseudospectral method
"k-Wave uses a pseudospectral method where spatial derivatives are computed by taking the FFT of the pressure field into k-space, multiplying by ik — which is the exact Fourier representation of differentiation — and then inverse-transforming back. This is exact with no numerical approximation, unlike finite differences which accumulate phase error over thousands of time steps. A k-space correction operator κ equal to sinc of c_ref times k times Δt over 2 is applied at each time step, which eliminates numerical dispersion for homogeneous media. For heterogeneous media like skull, the correction is approximate but still dramatically better than finite differences. The result is that k-Wave requires only about 2 to 3 grid points per wavelength instead of 10 or more, making skull simulations both accurate and computationally feasible."

Layer 5 · Targeting

Targeting, Aberration & Phase Correction

How anatomical coordinates become simulation grid indices, why the skull deflects the focus, and the time-reversal method that snaps it back to the target.

Converting real-world coordinates to grid indices

grid_index = round(physical_mm / dx_mm) + offset

BabelBrain does this automatically from MNI coordinates. Raw k-Wave: target 40 mm deep, transducer at col 10 → col = 10 + round(40/0.5) = 90

arc_pos — transducer centre
[row, col] grid index

Geometric centre of the transducer arc on the grid. In real lab: scalp entry point from BabelBrain / K-Plan divided by dx. Stays fixed throughout correction — you never move the transducer to fix aberration.

focus_pos — geometric target
[row, col] grid index

Tells makeArc how to curve the arc — the centre of curvature. In real lab: thalamus centroid from 3D Slicer → MNI mm → grid index via affine transform. Skull aberration overrides this during propagation.

Time-reversal correction pipeline
1
Forward simulation — observe aberrated focus

Run Simulation 2. Find where p_max is highest — that is where the skull moved the focus. Compare to focus_pos to measure the shift.

[~,idx] = max(sensor_data.p_max(:)); [row_f, col_f] = ind2sub([Nx Ny], idx);
2
Time-reversal — place point source at TRUE target

Swap source and sensor. Point source at focus_pos (thalamus), sensor at transducer positions. Wave travels backward through skull.

source_TR.p_mask(focus_pos(1), focus_pos(2)) = 1;   sensor_TR.mask = source.p_mask;
3
Phase conjugation — flip the received signal in time

The time-reverse of what arrived at the transducer, when re-emitted, retraces exactly back to the target through the skull.

corrected_drive = fliplr(data_TR.p);  % or: -angle(fft(...)) in frequency domain
4
Corrected forward — only source.p changes

Same arc_pos. Same focus_pos. Same medium. Same grid. Only source.p carries corrected per-element phases. Focus snaps to target.

source.p = corrected_drive;  % N_elements × Nt matrix with phase corrections
What to change when the focus misbehaves
→ Change source.p — apply per-element phase delays via time-reversal. Do NOT move arc_pos. Moving the transducer shifts the geometric aim crudely without correcting per-element phase errors — the focus stays blurred even if it moves closer to target.
→ Change source.p amplitudes — apply apodisation (weight outer elements less) plus phase correction. Large FWHM means partial destructive interference at focus due to unequal skull thickness across aperture.
→ Scale up source.p amplitude (check safety limits first). Or consider moving arc_pos to a thinner skull region (temporal window). This is the ONE case where transducer repositioning is justified — not for phase correction but for acoustic window quality.
→ Change arc_pos to a different scalp entry point (e.g. temporal vs vertex window). BabelBrain/K-Plan help identify the optimal entry point by simulating multiple positions.
★ FWHM measurement from sensor_data

FWHM = Full Width at Half Maximum — the width of the pressure profile where it drops to 50% of peak. Quantifies focal spot size. Typical tFUS values: lateral FWHM 3–8 mm, axial FWHM 10–30 mm.

p_axial  = sensor_data.p_max(Nx/2, :);
half_max = max(p_axial) / 2;
above    = find(p_axial >= half_max);
FWHM_mm  = (above(end) - above(1)) * dy * 1000;

Nx = roundUpToFFTSize(N_min_x);  % k-Wave helper
Ny = roundUpToFFTSize(N_min_y);  % much faster than 2^nextpow2
Transducer source types
source.p_mask = output of makeArc (2D) or makeBowl (3D). source.p = 1×Nt. One waveform drives all mask points equally. Used with single-channel signal generators (Sonic Concepts H317). Natural focus determined by radius of curvature.
Load element position file (N_elem × 3 matrix of [x,y,z] mm). Convert each to grid index: ix = round(pos_mm(n,1)/dx/1000) + Nx/2. Set that point in source.p_mask = 1. source.p becomes N_elem × Nt — each row a phase-corrected tone burst. Enables steering and skull correction.
Single grid-point sources radiate isotropically — incorrect for finite-area elements. Off-grid sources use sinc-weighted spreading across neighbouring grid points, correctly representing a bandlimited source on a coarse grid. source.p_mask becomes a weighted float matrix (not just 0s/1s). BabelBrain implements this automatically.
Single point at target: sensor.mask(tx,ty) = 1 — full time series, minimal memory. For waveform analysis.

Line through focus: sensor.mask(Nx/2, :) = 1 — axial pressure profile for FWHM measurement.

Volume ROI box: ±10 mm around target, record p_max/p_min/p_rms — for 3D focal maps and safety metrics.

Full grid: ones(Nx,Ny) — 2D only. In 3D: 256³ p_max map = 67 MB acceptable, full time series = ~25 GB — never in 3D.

ISPTA vs ISPPA: ISPTA averages intensity over the full pulse repetition period (including OFF time). ISPPA averages over just the ON pulse. ISPTA is always ≤ ISPPA × duty cycle. Interviewers often ask you to distinguish these — ISPTA is the thermal safety metric; MI is the cavitation safety metric.

Full guide

Seven layers from foundation to advanced use

01

Acoustic Wave Physics

k-Wave models pressure waves through first-order acoustic equations: conservation of momentum, conservation of mass, and the pressure-density relation. Students should be able to explain pressure, particle velocity, density, and sound speed before touching code.

02

Heterogeneous Medium

Real tissue is voxel-wise. `medium.sound_speed`, `medium.density`, `medium.alpha_coeff`, and `medium.alpha_power` may be scalars or maps. Impedance `Z = rho c` explains reflection at water-skull and skull-brain boundaries.

03

k-Space Pseudospectral Method

Spatial derivatives are computed with FFT-based spectral accuracy, while the k-space correction improves time stepping for propagating waves. This is why grid spacing, points per wavelength, and CFL matter.

04

MATLAB Simulation Setup

The core mental model is four structs: `kgrid` defines space and time, `medium` defines tissue, `source` launches energy, and `sensor` records what happened.

05

Targeting and Aberration

The skull delays, reflects, and attenuates the wave. Time reversal and phase conjugation convert those delays into source corrections for focused ultrasound.

06

Advanced Simulation Decisions

Move from examples to research by choosing PML size, grid resolution, absorption law, GPU or C++ acceleration, sensor outputs, and validation tests deliberately.

07

From Patient Images to k-Wave

A lab pipeline often starts with CT or MRI, segmentation, HU-to-acoustic conversion, registration, source placement, simulation, and reporting of pressure, intensity, and focus metrics.

Code literacy

The four objects students must master

kgrid

Physical grid size, spatial step, time array, and CFL-limited temporal sampling.

kgrid = kWaveGrid(Nx, dx, Ny, dy);
kgrid.makeTime(medium.sound_speed);

medium

Sound speed, density, attenuation, nonlinearity, and tissue maps.

medium.sound_speed = c_map;
medium.density = rho_map;
medium.alpha_coeff = alpha_map;

source

Initial pressure, time-varying pressure, transducer mask, phase, and amplitude.

source.p_mask = transducer_mask;
source.p = toneBurst(1/kgrid.dt, f0, cycles);

sensor

Where data is recorded: points, lines, planes, masks, or whole pressure fields.

sensor.mask = detector_mask;
sensor.record = {'p', 'p_max', 'I_avg'};
