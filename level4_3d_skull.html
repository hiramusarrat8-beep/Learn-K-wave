IT'IS database values used by every clinical tFUS lab
Water / coupling gel
c (m/s)1500
ρ (kg/m³)1000
α (dB/MHz/cm)~0
Z (MRayl)1.5
Brain (grey matter)
c (m/s)1560
ρ (kg/m³)1046
α (dB/MHz/cm)0.6
Z (MRayl)1.63
Cortical skull bone
c (m/s)2900
ρ (kg/m³)1900
α (dB/MHz/cm)10–15
Z (MRayl)5.51
⚠️
The impedance mismatch explains everything Skull Z = 5.51 MRayl, brain Z = 1.63 MRayl. The reflection coefficient at the skull-brain interface: R = (Z₂−Z₁)/(Z₂+Z₁) = (1.63−5.51)/(1.63+5.51) = −0.54. That's 54% of pressure reflected, plus Snell's law refraction of the transmitted portion. This is why tFUS is hard and why we simulate.
2
HU → acoustic properties (Aubry 2003 formula)
The most cited formula in tFUS simulation literature
Aubry 2003 — CT Hounsfield Units to acoustic properties
porosity = 1 - (HU / 1000) % 0=dense bone, 1=water
c = 2900 - 1480 × porosity % m/s (2900→1420)
ρ = 1900 - 900 × porosity % kg/m³ (1900→1000)
α = 13.3 × porosity + 0.17 % dB/(MHz^y cm)
📖
Aubry vs Marsac vs Bancel — which to use? Aubry 2003 is the original and most cited. Marsac 2017 and Bancel 2021 use direct linear HU scaling (what BRIC uses) — simpler but different empirical fit. All three give similar results for tFUS planning. Your site teaches Aubry because it exposes the porosity concept explicitly, which is more physically intuitive.

% HU map: Nx×Ny×Nz matrix of CT values
HU.skull(HU.skull < 300) = 0; % threshold: only bone HU≥300
HU thresholding — only use bone
Soft tissue (brain, skin, fat) has HU near 0–100. Setting values below 300 to zero means those voxels are treated as water in the Aubry formula. Only cortical bone (HU 300–2000) gets bone-like acoustic properties. This is standard practice — BRIC uses the same threshold.
HU.skull(HU.skull > 2000) = 2000; % cap at max bone HU
porosity = 1 - (HU.skull / 1000);
Porosity — the physical meaning
Porosity is the fractional volume of the voxel that is NOT bone (i.e., filled with marrow or water). Dense cortical bone: HU=1000 → porosity=0 (pure bone). Trabecular bone: HU=500 → porosity=0.5 (half bone, half marrow). Water: HU=0 → porosity=1. The Aubry formula linearly mixes water and bone properties by porosity.
%% ── Step 2: Map to acoustic properties ───────────
medium.sound_speed = 2900 - 1480 .* porosity; % m/s
Sound speed — element-wise on 3D matrix
This single line computes c for every voxel simultaneously. At porosity=0 (pure bone): c=2900. At porosity=1 (pure water): c=2900−1480=1420 m/s (Aubry's water value — note this is slightly different from the 1500 we use for free water; the formula was calibrated for skull-adjacent tissue). The result is an Nx×Ny×Nz matrix.
c = c_bone × (1−porosity) + c_water × porosity
= 2900×(1−p) + 1420×p = 2900 − 1480p
medium.density = 1900 - 900 .* porosity; % kg/m³
Density — same mixing rule
Linear interpolation between bone (1900 kg/m³) and water (1000 kg/m³). Together, c and ρ give impedance Z=ρc at every voxel. Z gradients between adjacent voxels determine how much energy reflects vs transmits. Dense bone: Z=2900×1900=5.51 MRayl. Brain: Z=1560×1046=1.63 MRayl.
medium.alpha_coeff = 13.3 .* porosity + 0.17; % dB/MHz^y/cm
Alpha — counterintuitive direction
Notice: higher porosity → higher alpha. Dense bone (porosity=0) gets α=0.17, while water-like voxels (porosity=1) get α=13.47. This seems backwards at first — but Aubry's formula reflects the fact that trabecular bone (high porosity) absorbs more than dense cortical bone due to its irregular microstructure and marrow interfaces. This is empirically derived.
medium.alpha_power = 2.0; % single scalar — applies to all voxels
alpha_power is always a scalar
Even though alpha_coeff is an Nx×Ny×Nz matrix, alpha_power MUST be a single scalar. k-Wave applies one power law exponent to the entire medium. You cannot have different y values in different voxels. 2.0 is used for the Aubry skull model. For soft tissue alone you might use 1.5.
%% ── Step 3: Override non-skull voxels ────────────
brain_mask = (HU.skull == 0); % where HU was below threshold
Brain mask from the HU threshold
After thresholding in Step 1, all non-skull voxels have HU=0. The brain mask is simply where HU==0. These voxels will get IT'IS brain properties rather than the Aubry formula values, which would incorrectly assign them near-water properties.
medium.sound_speed(brain_mask) = 1560; % brain grey matter
IT'IS values — not Aubry
Brain tissue properties come from the IT'IS (International Telecommunication Union tissue properties) database — not from the Aubry formula. The Aubry formula only applies to skull bone. For brain: c=1560 m/s, ρ=1046 kg/m³, α=0.6 dB/MHz/cm (IT'IS values for grey matter at body temperature).
medium.density(brain_mask) = 1046; % kg/m³
medium.alpha_coeff(brain_mask) = 0.6; % dB/MHz^y/cm

% Create a synthetic skull slab with realistic HU values
% Skull: occupies x = 12–18 mm from the transducer
skull_thickness_mm = 6; % typical human parietal bone
Typical skull thickness
Adult parietal bone is 5–8 mm thick. A 6 mm synthetic skull at dx=0.5 mm = 12 grid points. Real patient skull varies by location: frontal bone (where most tFUS targets are) is typically 6–9 mm.
skull_start = round(12e-3 / dx); % grid index where skull starts
skull_end = skull_start + round(skull_thickness_mm*1e-3/dx);
HU_map = zeros(Nx, Ny, Nz); % start with all zeros (water)
HU=0 means water everywhere to start
Initialise the entire grid as HU=0 (water). Then fill in the skull region. The Aubry formula will convert HU=0 voxels to water properties and HU=700 voxels to bone properties. The transducer coupling medium (water) naturally gets the right properties.
% Dense cortical bone outer table: HU ~1000
HU_map(skull_start:skull_start+2, :, :) = 1000;
% Trabecular (diploe) interior: HU ~400–600 (lower density)
HU_map(skull_start+3:skull_end-3, :, :) = 500; % diploe
Three-layer skull structure
Real skull has three layers: (1) outer cortical table — dense cortical bone, HU ≈ 900–1200; (2) diploe — trabecular/spongy bone with marrow, HU ≈ 300–600 — this layer absorbs most ultrasound energy; (3) inner cortical table — dense bone, HU ≈ 800–1100. This 3-layer structure is why skull is such a challenging barrier for ultrasound.
% Dense cortical bone inner table: HU ~900
HU_map(skull_end-2:skull_end, :, :) = 900;
% Now run Aubry formula on HU_map → medium structs
Same code as real CT from here
Once you have HU_map (whether from a real CT via niftiread, or this synthetic version), the Aubry conversion code is identical. This is the key pedagogical point: your Level 4 learning applies directly to real patient data. The only difference in Level 5 is how HU_map is created.

checkStability — the step I omitted in Level 3
Essential when medium is heterogeneous. BRIC always does this.
🚨
Why heterogeneous media need this check In water, makeTime(c_water, 0.3) guarantees stability. But with skull (c_max=2900), the same dt that's safe for water may be too large. checkStability computes the exact stability limit for your specific heterogeneous medium and returns the maximum safe dt. Always use it when medium.sound_speed is a matrix.

% ── CFL rule with heterogeneous medium ─────────────
cfl = 0.3;
% ── IMPORTANT: use c_MAX for dt calculation ─────────
Always use c_max, never c_brain
The CFL condition must be satisfied for the fastest wave in the medium. If you compute dt from c_brain=1560 but skull has c=2900, the wave in the skull covers nearly twice the distance per time step — violating CFL and making the simulation unstable (you'll see exploding pressure values). Always compute dt from c_max = max(medium.sound_speed(:)).
dt = CFL × dx / c_max = 0.3 × 0.5e-3 / 2900 ≈ 52 ns
c_max = max(medium.sound_speed(:)); % = 2900 from skull
medium.sound_speed_ref — also set this
k-Wave uses a reference sound speed for the k-space operator κ = sinc(c_ref·k·dt/2). Set medium.sound_speed_ref = c_max explicitly. If you don't, k-Wave defaults to the mean, which can introduce phase errors in strongly heterogeneous media. BRIC and PRESTUS both set this.
medium.sound_speed_ref = c_max; % for k-space operator κ
Why sound_speed_ref matters
κ = sinc(c_ref·k·dt/2) is the k-space correction that eliminates numerical dispersion. For homogeneous media, setting c_ref = c gives exact phase correction. For heterogeneous media, c_ref is a single value applied globally — k-Wave recommends c_max to guarantee stability, accepting that phase correction is approximate in low-speed regions.
% ── Compute time array ──────────────────────────────
t_end = sqrt(kgrid.x_size.^2 + kgrid.y_size.^2) / 1500; % diagonal / c_water
dt = cfl * dx / c_max;
Nt = round(t_end / dt);
% ── checkStability: verify dt is safe ──────────────
dt_limit = checkStability(kgrid, medium);
checkStability — what it actually computes
checkStability computes the exact stability limit from the heterogeneous medium: dt_max = 2/(c_ref × k_max) × arcsin(c_ref/c_max). If your computed dt exceeds this limit, the simulation will diverge (pressures blow up to Inf/NaN). The function returns Inf for homogeneous media (unconditionally stable) or a finite dt_limit for strongly heterogeneous cases.
dt_limit = (2 / (c_ref × k_max)) × arcsin(c_ref / c_max)
if dt_limit ~= Inf && dt > dt_limit
Only override if needed
Inf means unconditionally stable — no need to change dt. Only override when dt_limit is finite AND your computed dt is too large. Using a smaller dt than necessary just makes the simulation slower (more time steps), not wrong. The BRIC code always overrides to dt_limit if it's finite, which is conservative but safe.
dt = dt_limit;
Nt = round(t_end / dt);
end
kgrid.setTime(Nt, dt);

%% ── Safety metrics ────────────────────────────────
p_peak = max(p_max(:)); % peak positive pressure [Pa]
% ISPPA at focus (brain tissue: ρ=1046, c=1560)
ISPPA — use brain tissue properties
ISPPA = p² / (2ρc) × 10⁻⁴ [W/cm²]. Use the local tissue properties at the focal point — brain, not skull. If you use skull properties (ρ=1900, c=2900), you'd underestimate ISPPA by a factor of ~3.6. The FDA limit is ISPPA < 190 W/cm² (diagnostic) or no limit for therapeutic, but ISPTA < 720 mW/cm² applies.
ISPPA [W/cm²] = p_peak² / (2 × 1046 × 1560) × 1e-4
rho_brain = 1046; c_brain = 1560; % IT'IS values
ISPPA = p_peak^2 / (2 * rho_brain * c_brain) * 1e-4; % W/cm²
% MI (mechanical index) — use p_min (negative peak)
MI uses negative peak pressure
MI = |p_min [MPa]| / √f₀ [MHz]. This requires recording p_min (peak negative pressure), not p_max. Add 'p_min_all' to sensor.record. Negative pressure drives cavitation — the FDA limit MI < 1.9 is designed to prevent this. For tFUS neuromodulation, typical MI is 0.1–0.5, well below the limit.
% Add 'p_min_all' to sensor.record to get p_neg
% MI = abs(p_min_MPa) / sqrt(f0_MHz)
f0_MHz = f0 * 1e-6; % convert Hz → MHz
MI = abs(p_peak) * 1e-6 / sqrt(f0_MHz); % approx — use p_min ideally
fprintf('ISPPA = %.1f W/cm² (limit: 190 W/cm²)\n', ISPPA);
fprintf('MI = %.2f (limit: 1.9)\n', MI);

clearvars; close all;
%% ═══ 1. GRID ════════════════════════════════════════
Nx=128; Ny=64; Nz=64; % elongated in x (propagation axis)
f0 = 500e3; c_water = 1500; c_skull = 2900;
dx = c_water / (6 * f0); % 6 PPW at brain speed → 0.5 mm
kgrid = kWaveGrid(Nx,dx,Ny,dx,Nz,dx);
%% ═══ 2. SKULL (SYNTHETIC CT) ═════════════════════════
HU_map = zeros(Nx,Ny,Nz); % all water to start
s1 = round(15e-3/dx); % skull starts at 15 mm from face
s2 = s1 + round(6e-3/dx); % skull 6 mm thick
HU_map(s1:s1+2, :, :) = 1000; % outer cortical table
HU_map(s1+3:s2-3, :, :) = 500; % diploe (trabecular)
HU_map(s2-2:s2, :, :) = 900; % inner cortical table
%% ═══ 3. MEDIUM (Aubry 2003) ══════════════════════════
porosity = 1 - (HU_map / 1000);
medium.sound_speed = 2900 - 1480 .* porosity;
medium.density = 1900 - 900 .* porosity;
medium.alpha_coeff = 13.3 .* porosity + 0.17;
medium.alpha_power = 2.0;
% Override brain/water voxels with IT'IS values
brain_mask = (HU_map == 0);
medium.sound_speed(brain_mask) = 1560;
medium.density(brain_mask) = 1046;
medium.alpha_coeff(brain_mask) = 0.6;
medium.sound_speed_ref = c_skull; % = c_max
%% ═══ 4. TIME ARRAY + STABILITY ═══════════════════════
cfl = 0.3;
dt = cfl * dx / c_skull; % use c_MAX
t_end = sqrt(kgrid.x_size^2+kgrid.y_size^2) / c_water;
Nt = round(t_end/dt);
dt_lim = checkStability(kgrid, medium);
if dt_lim ~= Inf && dt > dt_lim; dt=dt_lim; Nt=round(t_end/dt); end
kgrid.setTime(Nt, dt);
%% ═══ 5. SOURCE (same as Level 3) ═════════════════════
bowl_pos = [1, Ny/2+1, Nz/2+1];
focus_pos = [Nx/2, Ny/2+1, Nz/2+1];
radius = round(35e-3/dx); diameter = round(25e-3/dx);
if mod(diameter,2)==0; diameter=diameter+1; end
source.p_mask = makeBowl([Nx,Ny,Nz],bowl_pos,radius,diameter,focus_pos);
source.p = toneBurst(1/dt, f0, 5);
%% ═══ 6. SENSOR ═══════════════════════════════════════
sensor.mask = zeros(Nx,Ny,Nz);
sensor.mask(:,:,Nz/2+1) = 1; % z mid-plane
sensor.record = {'p_max_all'};
%% ═══ 7. GEOMETRY CHECK ═══════════════════════════════
voxelPlot(double(source.p_mask | (HU_map>300))); view([127,18]);
title('Bowl (active) + skull slab — check before running!'); pause(2);
%% ═══ 8. RUN ══════════════════════════════════════════
sensor_data = kspaceFirstOrder3D(kgrid,medium,source,sensor, ...
'DataCast','single', 'PMLInside',false, 'PlotPML',false);
%% ═══ 9. VISUALISE + SAFETY ═══════════════════════════
p_slice = reshape(sensor_data.p_max_all, Nx, Ny);
figure; imagesc(kgrid.y_vec*1e3,kgrid.x_vec*1e3,p_slice);
colormap(getColorMap); axis image; colorbar;
xlabel('y [mm]'); ylabel('x [mm]'); title('Peak pressure — 3D transcranial [Pa]');
p_peak = max(p_slice(:));
ISPPA = p_peak^2 / (2*1046*1560) * 1e-4; % W/cm²
MI = p_peak*1e-6 / sqrt(f0*1e-6);
fprintf('Peak: %.0f Pa | ISPPA: %.1f W/cm² | MI: %.2f\n',p_peak,ISPPA,MI);

Check yourself
Three questions before moving to Level 5
Question 1 of 3
You set medium.sound_speed from the Aubry formula, then compute dt = 0.3 × dx / 1500 (water speed). The simulation produces NaN pressure values. What went wrong?
The CFL condition requires dt ≤ CFL × dx / c_max. When skull has c=2900, computing dt from c=1500 gives a dt that is 2900/1500 = 1.93× too large. In heterogeneous media, always compute dt from max(medium.sound_speed(:)), then optionally verify with checkStability(kgrid, medium).
Question 2 of 3
After running the Level 4 simulation, your p_max map shows the peak pressure is 8 mm away from your intended focus_pos. What is the correct first response?
Focal shift is a physical effect of skull refraction — not a simulation error. The skull slows and bends the beam following Snell's law (c_skull=2900 → c_brain=1560 causes a large refraction angle). Level 3 had no shift because water is homogeneous. This is exactly why tFUS needs simulation: to predict the shift and correct for it via phase delays (aberration correction), which is the core of Level 5.
Question 3 of 3
medium.alpha_power = [2.0, 1.5] — you want skull to use y=2.0 and brain to use y=1.5. Will this work?
medium.alpha_power is hardcoded as a scalar in k-Wave. The power law exponent y applies to the entire medium. This is a fundamental limitation — you cannot have different y values per voxel. When BRIC uses y=1.43 (Robertson 2017) for the whole medium, it's an empirical compromise between the true skull exponent (~2.0) and the brain exponent (~1.5). Research into spatially varying alpha_power is an active area.
